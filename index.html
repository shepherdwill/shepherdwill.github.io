Dynamism in the UK economy

Will Shepherd | LinkedIn | GitHub

Dynamism, at it's loosest definition, can be considered as the rate of change in the economy. Historically, periods of high economic change have resulted in economic growth, technological advancement and increased living standards. In the UK, but also in the US and Europe, a slowdown in dynamism has been documented over the last 20 years. The economy becoming less dynamic has coincided with a period of stalling productivity growth. The two trends are likely linked.

The productivity context

The UK productivity slowdown has been widely documented. Since 2007, productivity growth has been extremely weak falling significantly below the trend line of past productivity performance.

Why should we be concerned about low productivity growth? It directly translates to stagnant real wages and prevents further increases in living standards. At a time when demands on public finances are increasing in advanced economies, low productivity growth also hampers tax revenues to pay for these rising obligations.

A number of explanations have been offered ...

  • Chronic underinvestment from both private and public sectors
  • The long tail of unproductive firms
  • Skill gaps and labour market issues
  • The legacy of the 2008 Financial Crisis

The productivity distribution across UK firms has become more dispersed over time, with a growing gap between the most and least productive firms. Whilst it is good news that the most productive firms are continuing to improve, these firms employ only a small share of the population meaning the benefits, such as higher wages, are not shared widely. Most firms have experienced little growth in productivity over the last 15 years. The median productivity level, representing the 'typical' firm, is the same in 2023 as it was in 2007.

The latest ONS data shows a remarkable increase in productivity for the top 10% of firms (90th percentile) between 2020 and 2023. Agriculture, Forestry and Fishing, Other Service, and Information and Communication saw the highest percentage increase in 90th percentile productivity from 2020-2023. These firms may have achieved higher productivity through superior investments, adoption of advanced digital technology, better management practices or in some cases from market power.

If the productivity gains shown by the top 10% of firms could be replicated by others, a process known as diffusion, the benefits of productivity growth could be shared wider. This would increase aggregate productivity growth considerably. A dynamic economy encourages the diffusal process by providing competitive pressure for companies to innovate and improve processes, whilst the most unproductive firms exit the market.

What does dynamism mean?

Business dynamism describes the constant and healthy churning of an economy as new businesses are created, existing ones expand or contract, and others close down. A dynamic economy is one where resources like people, ideas, and money are constantly moving to where they can be most productive.

Most research focuses on the movement of labour between firms, it is the easiest resource to measure. Total job reallocation measures the total number of jobs being added or taken away by firms, giving an idea of how much firms are adjusting their labour. If fewer firms are hiring/firing the total number of jobs reallocated will be fewer. In other words, the economy is less dynamic.

After four consecutive years of falling reallocation, the job reallocation rate increased in 2022 and remained elevated in 2023 before falling slightly in 2024. This increase was driven primarily by existing firms creating jobs at a higher rate.

Micro firms with 0-9 employees are contributing the most to job reallocation in the UK economy. In 2024, 2% of jobs were destroyed by Micro firms whilst 3.5% were destroyed in total. The smallest firms were responsible for 57% of job destruction.

Large firms, however, contribute the most to job creation. In 2024, 3.9% of jobs were created by existing large firms. That makes up more than half of the jobs created by existing firms that year.
